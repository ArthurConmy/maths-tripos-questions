4.II.7E

#Numbers and Sets
2006

State and prove Fermat's Little Theorem.

An odd number nnn is called a Carmichael number if it is not prime, but every positive integer aaa satisfies an≡a( mod n)a^{n} \equiv a(\bmod n)an≡a(modn). Show that a Carmichael number cannot be divisible by the square of a prime. Show also that a product of two distinct odd primes cannot be a Carmichael number, and that a product of three distinct odd primes p,q,rp, q, rp,q,r is a Carmichael number if and only if p−1p-1p−1 divides qr−1,q−1q r-1, q-1qr−1,q−1 divides pr−1p r-1pr−1 and r−1r-1r−1 divides pq−1p q-1pq−1. Deduce that 1729 is a Carmichael number.

[You may assume the result that, for any prime ppp, there exists a number g prime to ppp such that the congruence gd≡1( mod p)g^{d} \equiv 1(\bmod p)gd≡1(modp) holds only when ddd is a multiple of p−1p-1p−1. The prime factors of 1729 are 7,13 and 19.]
