B2.5

#Combinatorics
2003

Prove Ramsey's theorem in its usual infinite form, namely, that if N(r)\mathbb{N}^{(r)}N(r) is finitely coloured then there is an infinite subset M⊂NM \subset \mathbb{N}M⊂N such that M(r)M^{(r)}M(r) is monochromatic.

Now let the graph N(2)\mathbb{N}^{(2)}N(2) be coloured with an infinite number of colours in such a way that there is no infinite M⊂NM \subset \mathbb{N}M⊂N with M(2)M^{(2)}M(2) monochromatic. By considering a suitable 2-colouring of the set N(4)\mathbb{N}^{(4)}N(4) of 4 -sets, show that there is an infinite M⊂NM \subset \mathbb{N}M⊂N with the property that any two edges of M(2)M^{(2)}M(2) of the form ad,bca d, b cad,bc with a<b<c<da<b<c<da<b<c<d have different colours.

By considering two further 2-colourings of N(4)\mathbb{N}^{(4)}N(4), show that there is an infinite M⊂NM \subset \mathbb{N}M⊂N such that any two non-incident edges of M(2)M^{(2)}M(2) have different colours.
